Part 1: Data

Observations for the BRFSS data analysis are collected in a cross-selectional uniform manner via telephone survey by landline or cellphone conducted by the state health department monthly. The number of interviews within each state differs based on funding and size of region. To be a selected region there had to be 500 adult respondents or more in the 2011 BRFSS combined for cellphone and landline data. Contrary to conducting a cellphone user participant, the landline telephone survey selects a random adult living within the household to participate. Prior to 2011, the statistical method that was used was known as post stratification to weight BRFSS survey data. Currently a new methodology called iterative stratification is used which allows more demographic variables to be considered to reduce bias and increase the overall representation of the population. However, this study is observational in nature and only correlation can be evaluated hence, causality cannot be implied. And since this study collected data via telephone surveys non-response bias must be considered.

Below is an overview of the demographics in this data set

According to the above bar plots, there are a higher number of participants who are females than males in all age categories. Also, the majority of the participants are white and older than 65 years old.

I created a box plot showing the numerical summaries that were calculated above. I chose a box plot since there are many outliers in the data and the mean and SD would be most affected by those outliers. Box plots are useful graphically because by examining the length of the box which represents the range among the middle 50% of the data (IQR = Q3-Q1) we can see where a majority of the data values lie. Also by examining where the top part of the box lies we can see where 75% of the data lies.

ggplot(MH_income, aes(x=income2, y=menthlth, fill = income2))+ geom_boxplot() + scale_y_continuous(limits=c(0, 30), name = "The Number of Mental Health Days in a Month") + scale_x_discrete(name="Classes of Income") + ggtitle("The Number of Mental Health Days Among Income Classes") + facet_grid(. ~ sex) + labs(fill = "Income Levels") + theme(axis.text.x=element_blank())

The above side-by-side box plots graphically summarizes the 5 statistics (min, Q1, median, Q3, max) of the reported mental health days per month among income classes for males and females. Overall, there is visual pattern between the number of mental health days reported and levels of income. As the level of income increases the number of mental health days per month reported decreases. Perhaps those with more income have less monetary stress than those of lower income classes. When comparing the side-by-side box plot graphs for males and females we can see the female side-by-side box plot tends to be higher than their male counterparts. These results are not surprising as females tend to be more susceptible to having mental health days than males. However, we cannot conclude any results without appropriate statistical testing.

Without statistical testing we cannot conclude the significance of the findings but perhaps men who are in a relationship or married tend to have less of a diabetes diagnosis than those who are divorced, separated, or became a widow.

According to the side-by-side bar plots, there may exist a relationship between the type of chronic disease someone has and the proportion of getting a flu shot although further statistical testing would be warranted. The proportions of getting a flu shot was highest among those who were diagnosed with arthritis (AR), cancer (CR), diabetes (DM), heart disease (HD), or kidney disease (KD) compared to those with lung disease (LD) and mental health (MH). It is interesting to point out that lung disease and mental health had very similar proportions between getting the flu shot and not receiving it. Among the groups that had higher proportions of getting the flu shots, it is interesting to point out that most of these groups would be those who would suffer from complications if they were to contract the flu. Perhaps if we were to categorize lung disease separately into COPD and asthma we may see a higher proportion in the number of Yes’s belonging to the COPD group since that disease state could have a higher impact on complications with the flu compared to those having some form of asthma.
